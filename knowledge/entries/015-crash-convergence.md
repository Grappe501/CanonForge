# Entry 015: Crash Convergence

If you mean the 2004 film Crash, then yes—it offers another storytelling technique we can borrow, but for a different reason than *The Sixth Sense*.
Here's how I'd think about it:
### The Sixth Sense
**Technique:** Recontextualization
You discover that what you believed about the story was wrong. The same scenes suddenly mean something different.
**Lesson for us:** Hide the truth in plain sight.
---
### Crash
**Technique:** Convergence
Multiple seemingly separate stories gradually reveal that they are interconnected. Small actions ripple across different lives, and the audience realizes everyone has been affecting everyone else.
**Lesson for us:** Every character can unknowingly become part of the same larger mechanism.
---
### What if we combine them?
Now we have something much more interesting:
* **Crash** gives us the *network*.
* **The Sixth Sense** gives us the *reveal*.
* **Lost** gives us the *breadcrumbs*.
* **A Princess of Mars**, **Buck Rogers**, **Flash Gordon**, and **Star Wars** give us the sense of epic adventure.
The result is a story where:
* Every character is connected.
* Every event matters.
* Every object has a history.
* Every conversation has two meanings.
* Every chapter belongs somewhere else in time.
Then, at the end, the reader realizes they haven't just misunderstood **one character**—they've misunderstood **the entire web of relationships**.
## One idea I absolutely love
Instead of one protagonist, imagine **five protagonists**.
The reader believes they're following five different people across different worlds and times.
At the end of the first read, they discover all five stories are actually one continuous life seen from different points in a fractured timeline.
Or, if we choose a different direction, they discover the five protagonists are distinct people whose lives have been intertwined by the same temporal fracture in ways neither they nor the reader fully understood.
Either approach creates enormous opportunities for layered revelations without relying on coincidence.
## My recommendation
I think our "recipe" should be something like this:
* **Adventure and wonder** from the classic planetary romances.
* **Interconnected lives** inspired by *Crash*.
* **Hidden clues** inspired by *Lost*.
* **Perspective-shifting revelation** inspired by *The Sixth Sense*.
* **A completely original mythology and time architecture** that belongs only to *Chronoforge*.
That combination feels fresh because we're borrowing **storytelling techniques**, not plots or characters. The result can stand on its own while giving readers the same sense of discovery that made those works memorable.
