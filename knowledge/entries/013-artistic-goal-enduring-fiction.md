# Entry 013: Artistic Goal Enduring Fiction

I actually think that's the stronger artistic goal.
The best examples of enduring fiction rarely became classics because readers were told how clever they were. They became classics because people finished them and couldn't stop talking about them.
That means the book has to earn its reputation organically.
### If that's your goal, I'd change our design philosophy.
Instead of writing:
> "A book with five hidden layers."
We write:
> "The best adventure story we can possibly tell."
The five layers become invisible engineering.
The reader should never think:
> "This author is trying to be clever."
They should think:
> "I have to know what happens next."
Only after the ending should they realize:
> "...I completely misunderstood what I was reading."
That's a very different experience.
### I'd make one rule absolutely sacred:
> **Never sacrifice the story for the puzzle.**
If a clue weakens a scene...
...the clue loses.
If a puzzle slows the pacing...
...the puzzle loses.
If symbolism makes dialogue unnatural...
...the symbolism loses.
Story always wins.
### Think of it like architecture.
No one walks into a cathedral because they're excited about buttresses.
They walk in because it's beautiful.
The engineering is what makes the beauty possible.
Our hidden architecture should function the same way.
### I think we should avoid this entirely
Don't tell readers:
* "Read it five times."
* "There are hidden clues."
* "The timeline is scrambled."
* "Watch for symbolism."
Nothing.
Let the ending do the work.
Imagine someone finishes the novella at 10:30 p.m.
They sit there for a minute.
Then they flip back to page one.
Not because you asked them to.
Because they *need* to.
That's the reaction we're chasing.
### That means our first page becomes incredibly important.
The first sentence has to be completely honest.
It also has to become almost unrecognizable in meaning by the fifth reading.
Not because the words changed.
Because the reader changed.
### One thought that keeps coming back to me
I almost don't want this to feel like science fiction.
I want it to feel like an old adventure novel.
Clean prose.
Strong characters.
Simple dialogue.
Fast pacing.
Like something that could have sat beside *A Princess of Mars* or *Treasure Island* on a shelf.
The complexity stays underneath.
Like a Swiss watch hidden inside a pocket watch.
---
I think we should adopt one motto for the entire project:
> **The reader should discover the genius, never be shown it.**
If we succeed at that, then the book won't need marketing based on its structure. People who finish it will become its marketers because they'll want someone else to experience the same realization they did. That's a much harder standard to meet—but if it works, it's also far more enduring.
