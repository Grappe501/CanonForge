# Entry 034: Reader Assigned Identity Article Xvii Assumption

Lets not assign any of those identities at all in the novella. Can we intentionally allow the reader to assign his own identity?>

Yes—and I think this could become one of the novel's most powerful techniques.
The key is that **the text simply doesn't specify certain characteristics unless they're genuinely necessary to the story.** That lets readers naturally construct mental images from their own experiences. Later, as new information arrives, they may discover that some of those assumptions were never supported by the text.
The important distinction is this:
* **Don't deliberately mislead readers by implying false attributes.**
* **Simply leave many attributes unstated.**
That keeps the storytelling fair while inviting readers to participate in the act of imagining.
## What we intentionally leave undefined
Unless the story truly requires it, we avoid specifying:
* Race or skin color.
* Nationality.
* Religion.
* Political beliefs.
* Physical attractiveness.
* Exact age (beyond what matters to the story).
* Accent.
* Socioeconomic status.
Instead, readers build those details themselves.
## The reveal isn't about appearance
I'd avoid a final reveal like, "The character looked different than you imagined."
That's interesting once, but it's small.
The deeper reveal is:
> "I realized how many assumptions I made without the author ever telling me."
That becomes an experience in self-awareness rather than a trick.
## A narrative rule
I would add this to the Constitution:
> **The novel never assigns an identity when the story does not require one.**
If a character's profession matters, we state it.
If their courage matters, we show it.
If their compassion matters, we demonstrate it.
If their ancestry doesn't matter to the story, we don't mention it.
## Where we should be careful
Some stories genuinely require specific cultural, historical, or personal identities because they shape the events. In those cases, we shouldn't erase them simply to maintain ambiguity.
For *Chronoforge*, though, your concept seems different. The story's central question is about perception, memory, and shared humanity. That makes selective ambiguity a natural fit.
## I think we can go one step further
Imagine that every major character is introduced primarily through:
* what they choose,
* what they fear,
* what they sacrifice,
* what they love,
* what they regret.
Readers meet the person before they meet any labels.
That reverses the order in which we often form impressions in everyday life.
## I think this deserves its own law
**Article XVII — Assumption**
> The novel provides readers with only the information necessary to understand the story.
>
> Where identity is irrelevant, the novel remains silent.
>
> Readers are free to imagine.
>
> The novel neither confirms nor denies those imaginings unless the story itself requires it.
>
> The purpose is not to conceal identity, but to reveal how readily we complete incomplete pictures.
I think this aligns beautifully with your larger vision. The novel wouldn't tell readers, "Don't judge people by labels." Instead, it would quietly let them discover how much of another person they had filled in on their own—and invite them to reconsider that habit long after they close the book.
