# Entry 023: Experience Product Business Model (Reaffirmation)

> Verbatim re-upload · content identical to CF-ENTRY-019 · author reaffirms Master Canon placement

I actually think what you just said changes the business model as much as it changes the creative goal.
You didn't say:
> "I want to sell a million copies."
You said:
> **"I want to give it away."**
That tells me the book itself isn't the product.
The **experience** is.
Think about the works that became cultural phenomena. People didn't recommend them because they admired the craftsmanship. They recommended them because they wanted someone else to have the same experience.
Not:
> "You should read this."
But:
> "Call me when you finish."
That's a completely different kind of recommendation.
## I think we need to define what success looks like.
Not reviews.
Not awards.
Not sales.
Success is this:
A reader finishes the last page.
They sit silently for two minutes.
They reopen the book.
Before they reach page five, they text three people.
Not because they understood everything.
Because they **need someone else to experience it**.
That's viral in the oldest, most human sense: sharing wonder.
---
## I think we should build for conversations.
Imagine this:
> "What did you think the compass meant?"
"What compass?"
"...Exactly."
Or:
> "Wait...you thought Chapter 11 happened first?"
"No..."
"You need to read it again."
Now readers aren't just consuming the story. They're helping each other uncover it.
---
## Here's one thing I would *not* do.
I would never tell readers there are five layers.
I wouldn't even hint that they're "supposed" to reread it.
Let them discover that naturally.
If the book earns a second reading, you've succeeded.
If it demands one, you've failed.
---
## Another idea
I wouldn't hide one giant secret.
I'd hide **hundreds of tiny certainties**.
Each time the reader says:
> "Oh...I assumed that."
They're learning something about themselves.
The book isn't exposing its secrets.
It's exposing the reader's assumptions.
---
## Here's the part that excites me most.
The ending shouldn't answer:
> "What happened?"
It should answer:
> "Why did I think that happened?"
Those are completely different questions.
One is about the story.
The other is about the reader.
---
## If we really commit to this...
...then I think our project has a different mission.
We're not trying to create:
* the smartest novel,
* the biggest twist,
* the most complicated timeline.
We're trying to create **a shared human experience**.
The kind where strangers who have never met immediately have something meaningful to talk about because they both went through the same journey.
---
### One thought I'd put in the Master Canon
> **The novel should never ask the reader to admire the author.**
>
> **It should invite the reader to admire their own capacity to discover.**
That may sound subtle, but it's a profound difference.
If readers finish thinking, "The author is a genius," they'll recommend *you*.
If they finish thinking, "I can't believe what *I* just realized," they'll recommend the *experience*.
And experiences are what people most eagerly share.
