# Entry 024: Anonymous Authorship And Teaches Rereading Mission

Now that's interesting.
An anonymous author isn't just a marketing decision—it could become part of the artistic statement.
But I think there's a right reason and a wrong reason to do it.
The wrong reason is to create mystery around the author.
The right reason is to remove the author from the conversation entirely.
If readers don't know who wrote it, they can't lean on:
* "This is what the author meant."
* "I read an interview where..."
* "The author confirmed..."
They're forced to stay inside the book itself.
That aligns remarkably well with what you've been describing.
---
## It also changes how readers interact.
Imagine this sequence:
Someone gives the novella to a friend.
The cover has only the title.
No author biography.
No endorsements.
No explanation.
No "Read this twice."
Just the story.
The friend finishes it.
Turns back to page one.
Then asks:
> "Who wrote this?"
And the answer is:
> "I don't know."
That creates a different kind of curiosity.
---
## I would even go further.
Don't make the author anonymous as a gimmick.
Make it part of the philosophy.
The work should stand on its own.
If readers begin hunting for clues about the author's identity instead of discussing the novel, we've accidentally shifted the focus away from the experience.
---
## Here's another idea.
The title page simply says:
> **CHRONOFORGE**
Nothing else.
At the end of the book, there is no biography.
No acknowledgments.
No explanation.
Just one final page.
Perhaps something as simple as:
> **Begin again.**
Or maybe even nothing at all.
The silence becomes part of the ending.
---
## One caution
If you eventually want traditional publication, complete anonymity can complicate contracts, copyright registration, royalties, and legal matters. You can absolutely publish under a **pseudonym**, while your real identity remains known to your publisher and protected legally. That gives you nearly the same reader experience without creating legal obstacles.
---
## One thing I think we should avoid
I don't want readers wondering:
> "Who wrote this?"
I want them wondering:
> "How did this book know I would think that?"
That's a much deeper reaction.
---
### My favorite thought from this entire brainstorming session
I think we've been describing the project in slightly the wrong way.
We're not trying to write a novel that people reread.
We're trying to write **a novel that teaches people how to reread**—not just books, but their own memories, assumptions, and lives.
That's a profoundly different ambition.
If we can stay true to that, then whether the cover carries a famous name, a pseudonym, or no name at all becomes secondary. The work itself becomes the conversation.
