# Entry 012: Commerce Sellability

Yes—but I'd frame the expectation carefully.
**Could we sell it to readers?** I think yes, if the execution is excellent.
**Could we sell it to a publisher?** Possibly, but it depends on how we position it.
The biggest risk isn't that it's "too complex." The biggest risk is that it becomes **a puzzle first and a novel second**. Most readers buy stories because they care about characters, not because they want to solve an intricate design.
### What makes it commercially promising
If the book succeeds on these three levels, it has real potential:
1. **A satisfying first read.** Someone who never rereads it should still feel they got a complete, emotionally rewarding story.
2. **A compelling hook.** "I just finished this book and realized the timeline wasn't what I thought. Now I have to read it again."
3. **Discussion value.** Readers start comparing theories, timelines, and clues. That kind of conversation can drive word-of-mouth.
Books that inspire readers to say, "You have to read this so we can talk about it," have a significant advantage.
### What would make it hard to sell
If the marketing has to explain the puzzle before anyone wants to read it, that's a warning sign.
A publisher or reader should be able to understand the appeal in one sentence, such as:
> "A time-fractured adventure where the ending reveals you've misunderstood the entire chronology—and each reread uncovers a deeper layer of the story."
That's intriguing without requiring a long explanation.
### How I'd approach publication
I'd think in stages:
* **Stage 1:** Write an exceptional 100–120 page novella.
* **Stage 2:** Get early readers to test whether the reread experience actually works.
* **Stage 3:** Refine based on where readers were confused versus delighted.
* **Stage 4:** Decide between traditional publishing and independent publishing based on the finished product.
A shorter novella also lowers the barrier for rereading, which is central to your concept.
### One idea that could make it stand out
Instead of advertising it as "a puzzle novel," market it as **a novel that changes every time you read it**.
That promise is easy to understand and naturally sparks curiosity.
### The key test
Before worrying about publishers, I'd set ourselves a much tougher internal goal:
> **Can we hand the manuscript to ten smart readers, and have at least seven immediately start reading it again after they finish?**
If that happens organically—without prompting—that's a very strong signal that the core concept is working.
I think the concept is distinctive enough to attract attention. The determining factor won't be the clever architecture; it'll be whether readers become emotionally invested in the characters while the hidden structure quietly does its work in the background. That's what turns an ingenious idea into a book people recommend.
